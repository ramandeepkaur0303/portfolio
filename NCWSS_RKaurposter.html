Exploring the Genetic Basis of Spine Evolution in Amaranthus

Background

Spines, thorns, and prickles represent a range of defensive structures co-evolved in many plants to deter herbivory and survive in diverse environments. Though these structures often serve similar functions, they differ in evolutionary origin: spines develop from leaf tissue and thorns from stem tissue, both containing vascular tissue, while prickles arise from epidermal or cortex tissue and lack vascular tissue.

This morphological diversity is reflected in various weedy species, with spines in Amaranthus spinosus (spiny amaranth) and Xanthium strumarium (common cocklebur), thorns in Cirsium vulgare (common thistle), and prickles in Rosa spp. (roses) — each adapted to specific ecological niches. These structures complicate manual weeding in agriculture and deter livestock grazing, presenting a significant nuisance in crop fields.

Methodology

Generation of Interspecific Mapping Population

To study the genetic basis of spine development in Amaranthus, two parental lines were selected: the non-spiny Amaranthus palmeri (Palmer amaranth) and the spiny Amaranthus spinosus (spiny amaranth). Crosses were performed in the PSL glass greenhouse in Urbana, Illinois, to produce F1 hybrids. Advanced backcross generations, such as BC3, will be developed to introgress spine-associated genomic regions from A. spinosus into the A. palmeri genetic background, creating populations segregating for the spine trait (Satterlee et al., 2024).

Figure 1: Crossbreeding between A. palmeri and A. spinosus.

Phenotyping and Genotyping of Backcross Populations

Phenotyping of hybrids and backcross progeny will be conducted during vegetative growth stages to identify spine traits. Following phenotyping, DNA will be extracted from leaf tissues of the segregating BC3 population using the SILEX protocol or an equivalent method (Vilanova et al., 2020). DNA quality and quantity will be evaluated using a NanoDrop spectrophotometer, and integrity will be confirmed via agarose gel electrophoresis. For genotyping, RFLP (Restriction Fragment Length Polymorphism) or GBS (Genotyping-by-Sequencing) techniques will be employed to associate spine traits with specific genomic intervals and distinguish between homozygous and heterozygous genotypes (Dai & Long, 2015; He et al., 2014). A fine-mapping approach, yet to be determined, will be used to further refine the loci associated with spine traits.

Figure 2: DNA extraction and quality assessment.

RNA Sequencing

To analyze gene expression, RNA will be extracted from leaf tissues of spiny and non-spiny individuals using TRIzol or a similar reagent. The RNA will be treated with DNase to remove any genomic DNA contamination, and its purity and concentration will be measured using a NanoDrop spectrophotometer. cDNA synthesis will be carried out using oligo-dT primers, and RT-qPCR will be performed with primers targeting candidate spine-related genes (Nolan et al., 2006). The expression data will be normalized using a housekeeping gene, and relative expression levels will be analyzed using the 2(-ΔΔCt) method to compare spiny and non-spiny individuals.

Comparative Genomic Analysis with Other Amaranthus Species

Comparative genomic analyses will be performed to explore the synteny, gene polymorphisms, and expression patterns of candidate spine-related genes across various Amaranthus species previously sequenced in the lab. These analyses will provide insights into the structural and functional variations of spine-associated regions and help to contextualize the evolutionary development of spiny traits within the genus.

To summarize, this study aims to uncover the genetic mechanisms underlying spine development in Amaranthus. By integrating phenotypic, genotypic, and transcriptomic analyses, the study will identify specific genomic regions, candidate genes, and regulatory elements associated with spine traits. Comparative genomic analyses will provide insights into the structural and functional variations of spine-related genes across Amaranthus species, shedding light on the evolutionary basis of spine development. Ultimately, this work will contribute to a deeper understanding of plant defense mechanisms and offer potential genetic targets for managing spiny traits in agricultural settings, with applications in weed control and breeding strategies.

Research Questions

  1. What are the key genetic differences between spiny and spineless Amaranthus species?
  2. Are there specific mutations that correlate with spine gain in cultivated Amaranthus species?
  3. Are there structural variants or mutations in the regulatory regions of these genes that lead to spine formation?

Did You Know?

Spines, thorns, and prickles are different! Although they may seem similar, these plant structures differ in their origin and anatomy. Spines are modified leaves, thorns are modified stems, and prickles are extensions of the cortex or epidermis. Understanding these differences is essential in botany and helps in studying plant defense mechanisms.
