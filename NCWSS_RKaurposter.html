Abstract

Plants have evolved a remarkable array of morphological defenses—such as spines, thorns, and prickles—to deter herbivores, reduce browsing pressure, and enhance survival in competitive environments. Although these structures share functional similarities, they diverge in developmental origins: spines are modified leaves, thorns arise from stems, and prickles emerge from epidermal or cortical tissues. Understanding the genetic and molecular underpinnings of these traits is essential for both ecological research and practical applications in agriculture.

Despite the importance of these defenses, the genetic pathways governing spine formation remain poorly characterized, particularly in weedy species. Recent studies in Solanum have implicated members of the LONELY GUY (LOG) gene family in prickle development, suggesting that key regulatory nodes may be conserved or co-opted across diverse taxa. However, comparable insights into weedy and agriculturally relevant genera like Amaranthus are lacking.

Here, we investigate the genetic architecture of spine evolution in Amaranthus through interspecific crosses between non-spiny A. palmeri and spiny A. spinosus. By integrating phenotypic assessment, high-throughput genotyping, transcriptomics, and comparative genomics, we aim to identify candidate loci, regulatory elements, and molecular pathways underlying spine formation. Our findings will illuminate the evolutionary dynamics of plant defenses and offer potential targets for breeding strategies that mitigate the agricultural challenges posed by spiny weeds.

Background

Defensive structures in plants have independently evolved multiple times, reflecting the diverse selective pressures that shape plant-herbivore interactions. In the genus Amaranthus, most species lack spines, yet A. spinosus produces prominent, sharp spines that confer ecological advantages in disturbed habitats. These spines reduce herbivory, complicate mechanical weeding, and deter grazing livestock, ultimately influencing plant fitness and weed management practices.

While morphological differences among spines, thorns, and prickles are well-documented, their genetic underpinnings remain elusive. Investigating the genomic architecture of spine development in Amaranthus not only broadens our fundamental understanding of plant defense evolution but also may reveal novel avenues for integrated weed management. Such insights could lead to targeted approaches that reduce spine prevalence, enhancing crop safety and easing manual removal efforts.

Methodology

Generation of Interspecific Mapping Populations

To dissect the genetic basis of spine formation, we performed controlled crosses between a non-spiny A. palmeri line and a spiny A. spinosus accession. The F1 hybrids, grown under greenhouse conditions at the University of Illinois, exhibit intermediate phenotypes. Advanced backcross populations (BC3) are being developed to generate segregating progeny that will refine the mapping resolution of spine-associated loci (Satterlee et al., 2024).

Phenotyping and Genotyping

Detailed phenotypic evaluations, including spine length, density, and distribution on stems and leaf nodes, are recorded at key vegetative stages. Genomic DNA from each individual is extracted using the SILEX method (Vilanova et al., 2020) to ensure high-quality samples. Subsequent genotyping-by-sequencing (GBS) and RFLP analyses identify polymorphic markers linked to spine-trait loci, enabling association mapping and QTL analyses (Dai & Long, 2015; He et al., 2014).

RNA Sequencing and Gene Expression Analysis

RNA is harvested from young leaf and stem tissues of spiny and non-spiny individuals for transcriptomic profiling. After cDNA synthesis, RT-qPCR quantifies the expression of candidate genes implicated in spine development, such as hormonal regulators and transcription factors. Normalization against stable housekeeping genes and comparison between phenotypes reveal expression patterns correlating with spine formation (Nolan et al., 2006).

Comparative Genomics

Comparative genomic analyses across multiple Amaranthus species assess syntenic regions, structural variations, and sequence polymorphisms within candidate loci. By examining conserved and divergent elements in spine-related genes, we gain insights into the evolutionary history of defense traits, identifying whether spine formation arose from lineage-specific innovations or the co-option of ancient developmental pathways.

Did You Know?

Spines, Thorns, and Prickles Are Distinct!
While they all serve as deterrents, spines are leaf-derived and contain vascular tissue, thorns originate from stem tissues, and prickles form as outgrowths of epidermal or cortical cells. Recognizing these differences helps us unravel the complex genetic networks and evolutionary histories that sculpt plant defenses.

Research Questions

  1. Which genetic loci and candidate genes underlie the transition from spineless to spiny phenotypes in Amaranthus?
  2. Do regulatory mutations, structural variants, or epigenetic modifications drive spine formation and diversification?
  3. How does the evolutionary trajectory of spine-related genes in Amaranthus compare to other plant lineages with defensive structures?
  4. Can insights from the genomic basis of spines inform weed management strategies and sustainable agricultural practices?
