Abstract

Plants have evolved a remarkable array of morphological defenses—such as spines, thorns, and prickles—to deter herbivores and enhance survival in diverse environments. Though functionally similar, these structures differ in their developmental origins: spines derive from leaf tissue, thorns from stems, and prickles from epidermal or cortical outgrowths. Understanding the genetics behind these structures is crucial for evolutionary biology and agriculture.

Despite their importance, the genetic underpinnings of spine formation remain unclear, especially in weedy species. In Amaranthus spinosus, a known agricultural weed, spines complicate manual weeding and discourage grazing. By leveraging interspecific crosses between non-spiny A. palmeri and spiny A. spinosus, we aim to pinpoint key genetic loci and regulatory networks responsible for spine formation.

Integrating phenotypic evaluations, genotyping-by-sequencing, RNA sequencing, and comparative genomics, our research will illuminate how spine-related genes evolve and function. The insights gained may guide strategies to manage spiny weeds and potentially engineer crops that are more resistant to herbivory or easier to cultivate.

Background

Defensive structures like spines have emerged multiple times throughout plant evolution. In Amaranthus, most species lack these features, making A. spinosus an intriguing model to study the genetic shifts involved. Spines not only offer ecological benefits by deterring herbivores but also pose significant challenges in agriculture.

Understanding the genetic basis for spine development can inform how such traits arise, diversify, and spread. This knowledge may prove valuable for weed management, reducing the labor costs and crop losses associated with spiny weeds. Moreover, unraveling the molecular controls of spine formation can offer insights into broader developmental processes in plants.

Methodology

Generation of Interspecific Mapping Populations

Controlled crosses between spiny A. spinosus and non-spiny A. palmeri lines yield F1 and advanced backcross (BC3) populations. These segregating populations allow us to map spine-associated loci with high resolution. The plants are grown under greenhouse conditions to ensure controlled environmental variables.

Phenotyping and Genotyping

Phenotypic assessments include measuring spine length, density, and spatial distribution. DNA is extracted using the SILEX protocol and subjected to genotyping-by-sequencing (GBS) for marker discovery. Identified polymorphisms are then correlated with spine traits to pinpoint candidate genomic regions.

RNA Sequencing and Expression Analysis

RNA from leaf and stem tissues of spiny and non-spiny individuals is extracted and sequenced. RT-qPCR validates candidate genes, revealing differential expression patterns that may underlie spine formation. Comparing transcriptomes provides insights into regulatory networks and gene co-option events that give rise to spines.

Comparative Genomics

Comparative analyses across multiple Amaranthus species identify conserved sequences, syntenic blocks, and structural variations related to spine evolution. This evolutionary perspective helps distinguish whether spine formation relies on ancient genetic frameworks or recent adaptive mutations.

Did You Know?

Spines, thorns, and prickles may look similar, but their distinct developmental origins reflect the diversity of plant defense strategies. Appreciating these differences enhances our understanding of how plants adapt to environmental pressures.

Research Questions

  1. Which genomic regions and candidate genes are associated with spine development in Amaranthus?
  2. Are spine traits controlled by regulatory mutations, structural variants, or gene co-option events?
  3. How do spine-related genes evolve and diversify across different Amaranthus species?
  4. Can we leverage genetic insights to guide sustainable weed management and improve agricultural productivity?
