Exploring the Genetic Basis of Spine Evolution in Amaranthus

Ramandeep Kaur1, Alexander J. Lopez1, Patrick J. Tranel1

1University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, Urbana, IL

Abstract

Spines, thorns, and prickles represent a range of defensive structures that have evolved in plants to deter herbivory and enable survival in diverse environments. While these structures often serve similar functions, they differ in their evolutionary origins and anatomical features. Spines derive from leaf tissue, thorns from stem tissue, and prickles from epidermal or cortex tissues.

Despite their ecological and agricultural significance—complicating weeding and deterring grazing—the genetic basis underlying the development of these structures remains poorly understood. Recent work in Solanum species revealed mutations in the LONELY GUY (LOG) gene family that influence prickle development. However, the genetics of spine formation in weedy species, such as Amaranthus spinosus, remains unknown.

This study investigates the genetic mechanisms driving spine development in Amaranthus, focusing on interspecific crosses between non-spiny A. palmeri and spiny A. spinosus. Using phenotypic assessments of segregating populations and high-throughput genotyping, we aim to identify loci associated with spine traits. Comparative genomic analyses across Amaranthus species will contextualize these findings, offering insights into the evolution of defensive traits and providing potential genetic targets for weed management strategies.

Background

Spines, thorns, and prickles have evolved multiple times in diverse plant lineages. For example, spines are present in Amaranthus spinosus and Xanthium strumarium, thorns in Cirsium vulgare, and prickles in Rosa spp. Each structure adapts to unique ecological niches, enhancing survival and deterring herbivores. In agriculture, spiny weeds complicate management and deter livestock grazing.

The genetic regulation of spine formation remains poorly understood in many weed species. Leveraging the genus Amaranthus provides an ideal model to uncover the genetic underpinnings of spine development due to the presence of both spiny and non-spiny species.

Methodology

Generation of Interspecific Mapping Populations

Crosses between non-spiny A. palmeri and spiny A. spinosus lines grown in a greenhouse at the University of Illinois produce F1 hybrids. Advanced backcross (BC3) populations are generated to segregate for spine traits (Satterlee et al., 2024).

Phenotyping and Genotyping

Plants are phenotyped during vegetative growth. DNA extraction (using SILEX; Vilanova et al., 2020) precedes genotyping-by-sequencing (GBS) or RFLP analysis to identify markers linked to spine development (Dai & Long, 2015; He et al., 2014).

RNA Sequencing and Gene Expression Analysis

RNA extraction, cDNA synthesis, and RT-qPCR quantification target candidate spine-related genes. Differential expression analysis identifies genes and regulatory elements involved in spine formation (Nolan et al., 2006).

Comparative Genomics

Comparisons of spine-related genomic regions across Amaranthus species examine synteny, structural variants, and polymorphisms, providing evolutionary context for spine development.

Did You Know?

Spines, Thorns, and Prickles Are Not the Same! Spines originate from leaf tissue, thorns from stems, and prickles from the epidermis/cortex. Understanding these differences is key to deciphering the evolution of plant defense mechanisms.

Research Questions

  1. What are the key genetic differences between spiny and spineless Amaranthus species?
  2. Are there specific mutations correlating with spine gain in cultivated and weedy Amaranthus species?
  3. How do structural variants or regulatory mutations influence the expression of spine-related genes?
