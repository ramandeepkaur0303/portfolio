My Story

Hello there! I'm Ramandeep Kaur, and I'm thrilled you dropped by! Currently, I'm pursuing my MS at South Dakota State University, USA. My research journey took an exciting turn when I stumbled upon the fascinating world of soyabean roots and grapewines. Getting amazing experience and chance to work with two amazing Scientist Dr Senthil Subramanian and Dr Anne Fennell, working on two different crops and a number of Projects.

Looking ahead, my dream is to contribute to Deciphering the complexities of Nature. A curious child I was turned into curious Women in STEM I am driven to find solutions through my expertise in genetics, bioinformatics, and molecular biology. With the amazing amalgamation of Computer Science and Molecular Biology, I aim to unlock the secrets of crop traits, genes, and cellular mechanisms that could revolutionize our approach to sustainable farming.

M.S Projects

Project 1: miRNA analysis for dormancy induction in Vitis riparia grapevines.
Abstract: Grapevine is a temperate fruit crop that may grow in a variety of regions worldwide. Temperate perennials employ dormancy as one strategy to deal with persistent environmental challenges. Dormancy has a substantial impact on grapevine flowering and vegetative growth in the spring and helps the vines withstand the chilly winter weather. For the bud break and typical spring development to take place the next season, vines must be subjected to adequate hours of cool temperature exposure during the dormant phase. This environmental phenomenon is utilized in greenhouse, by exposing the grapevines to short day photoperiod treatment, to induce dormancy and get deeper insights into it. Physiological and transcriptome studies in grapevine and other species have revealed that various molecular alterations are observed during dormancy induction. According to previous research conducted in lab, the gene expression of V. riparia witnesses a drastic change in response to short photoperiod (SD; 13 h) treatment between 14 and 42 days, which explains why grapevines undergo a considerable transition from the start of dormancy (14 days) till dormant state (42 days). Yet, just a few research have looked at how microRNAs (miRNAs) control bud dormancy induction. This study is performed on two vine siblings VRS-F2 110 and VRS-F2 040 at 14 (2 weeks) and 42 days (6 weeks) with a goal to analyze the quantity and expression profiles of miRNAs during dormancy and use the target genes of these miRNAs to characterize putative regulatory networks connected to dormancy. This study will integrate genes, transcription factors, and molecular processes together to provide us a far greater understanding of Vitis riparia dormancy.

Project 2: Gene Regulatory Networks associated with high- and low-capacity nitrogen-fixing rhizobia.
Abstract: This research proposal delves into the intricate realm of gene regulatory networks (GRNs) linked to high- and low-capacity nitrogen-fixing rhizobia in the context of soybean roots. GRNs are complex molecular systems that control gene expression, with a pivotal role played by transcription factors (TFs). Unraveling these networks is critical for understanding the genetic mechanisms governing soybean nodulation and nitrogen fixation.

Leveraging comprehensive transcriptomic data from split-root assays, this study harnesses computational modeling and data clustering to identify GRNs that are unique to the processes of soybean nodulation and nitrogen fixation. Co-expressed genes are organized into biclusters, shedding light on shared regulatory pathways that are active under specific conditions.

The research primarily focuses on the symbiotic relationship between soybeans and rhizobia, where nitrogen-fixing rhizobia establish themselves in soybean roots, obtaining nutrients while simultaneously providing fixed nitrogen to the plant. This symbiotic interaction is essential for soybean growth and holds significant agricultural importance.

Key methodologies encompass the following:

  • Aggregating data from transcriptomes to compile a list of TFs specific to root nodulation and nitrogen fixation.
  • Employing biclustering using Qubic software to identify co-expressed gene modules.
  • Utilizing Lemon-Tree and Inferelator, two module-based GRN inference methods, to infer GRNs.
  • Combining regulatory predictions to establish a consensus GRN.
  • Investigating potential protein-protein interactions (PPIs) via the STRING database.
  • Analyzing cis-regulatory motifs and functional enrichment to provide evidence of direct regulation.
  • This study promises to unravel the intricate web of gene regulation governing soybean nodulation and nitrogen fixation, offering valuable insights into enhancing crop productivity and sustainability.

Project 3: RNAseq analysis of shoot tip during photoperiod induced growth cessation in V. riparia grapevines.
Abstract: Short photoperiods usually cause growth cessation, cold adaptation, and dormancy induction in grapevines and other woody perennial plants native to temperate continental climes. Photoperiod-induced early activation of these mechanisms helps grapevines survive the winters in chilly temperate zones. Investigating the molecular processes in the shoot, will give researchers a better understanding of the signaling pathway that leads to growth arrest and dormancy induction. Vitis riparia Michx. grapevines that had grown for 35 days under a long photoperiod (long day, LD, 15 h) were given either a continuing LD or a short photoperiod (short day, SD, 10 h) treatment to begin studying photoperiod signal transduction. Shoot tips (10-node shoot terminals) were collected from each treatment at 7 and 21 days of LD and SD with 3 replicates of each treatment for RNASeq analysis. Differentially expressed genes were characterized in V. riparia shoot tips during active growth or SD induced growth cessation to examine physiological alterations in response to differential photoperiod treatments. The RNASeq analysis uncovered the signal transduction involved in V. riparia grapevine growth cessation and dormancy induction. Both 7SD and 7LD showed common growth pathways suggesting some level of similarity between the two. In 21SD, pathways involved in quenching reactive oxygen species were more abundant unlike LD tissues. In 7SD tissue, pathways involved in responding to stress conditions were more abundant than 21SD. Surprisingly, 21SD tissue was more abundant in pathways involving cell cycle and cell replication unlike 7SD.

Project 4: Promoter Analysis of differentially expressed genes between Vector control and STTM160 (miRNA 160 silenced) soybean roots.
Abstract: Legumes form root nodules to fix nitrogen through symbiotic relationship with nitrogen-fixing soil bacteria. In soybean, the root nodules form and function through hormonal balance of Auxin and Cytokinin. Auxin response factors (ARFs) regulate the auxin balance in soybean via miRNA 160 and 167, which silences ARF 16-2 and ARF 8a, accordingly. ARF 16-2 and ARF 8a binds to Auxin response elements (AuxREs) to repress and activate auxin gene expression respectively. Therefore, a balance between ARF8a and ARF 16-2 plays an important role in soybean nodule development. A RNASeq analysis was performed comparing auxin-responsive gene expression between control soybean roots and STTM160 roots (miRNA 160 silenced) to find genes and networks acting downstream of miR160 and miR167 during auxin response. miR160 was silenced using a short tandem target mimic (STTM160). Roots expressing a control construct (2C) and those expressing STTM160 were treated with a mock solution or auxin to identify auxin induced genes. The major gap in understanding the auxin response machinery is the identification of AuxRE motifs (transcription factors) recognized by ARF 16-2 and ARF 8a that control auxin gene expression. This study aims to evaluate promoters of these differentially expressed genes to identify distinct auxin response elements (AuxREs). We performed high-throughput promoter analysis to find AuxRE promoter motifs (TGTCNN) in differentially expressed genes and their binding affinities with ARF 16 and ARF 8a.

CitrusMarks: Citrus Marker Database

CitrusMarks is a database of DNA markers for citrus plants, including sweet orange, mandarin, and grapefruit. DNA markers are used to identify specific genetic variations that are associated with desirable traits, such as fruit flavor, color, and size. CitrusMarks contains information on thousands of markers, along with their physical positions on the genome, and can be used by breeders to develop new citrus varieties with improved characteristics.

Indian Medicinal Genome Database (Under Development)

The Indian Medicinal Genome Database is an innovative and comprehensive resource dedicated to the genomic study of Indian medicinal plants. This database is a treasure trove of genetic information, focusing on plants traditionally used in Ayurveda, Siddha, Unani, and other indigenous medicinal systems. It features a vast collection of genomic sequences, gene annotations, and DNA markers linked to therapeutic traits like healing properties, biochemical composition, and resistance to pests and diseases. Aimed at revolutionizing the field of phytomedicine, the database serves as a critical tool for researchers, herbalists, and pharmaceutical companies. It facilitates the exploration of genetic diversity, aids in the conservation of rare medicinal species, and supports the breeding of plants with enhanced medicinal qualities. The Indian Medicinal Genome Database is not just a platform for scientific discovery but also a step towards preserving and validating the rich heritage of Indian traditional medicine.
